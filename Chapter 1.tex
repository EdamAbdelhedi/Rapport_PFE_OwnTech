\chapter{Company Presentation and Internship Context}

\section{Company Overview}

OwnTech is an open-source power electronics initiative developed around the
idea of making power electronics more accessible, reusable, programmable, and
adaptable. Power electronics is a key technology for energy conversion: it is used
to interface photovoltaic panels, batteries, motors, electrical grids, embedded
systems, and many other energy sources or loads. However, the design of power
converters is usually complex because it combines hardware constraints,
real-time control, embedded software, measurement, safety, thermal management,
and electromagnetic compatibility.

\label{fig:owntech_logo}

The OwnTech structure combines an open-science foundation and an industrial
company. The OwnTech Foundation is placed under the aegis of the CNRS
Foundation and has existed since July 2022, while OwnTech Technologies was
created in October 2023. This organization makes it possible to keep the
project open and community-oriented while also supporting industrial
development and deployment. In the company presentation material, Mr. Jean
Alinei is presented as CEO of OwnTech Technologies, while Dr. Luiz Villa is
one of the co-founders of OwnTech. This structure illustrates the link between
engineering, research, and product development inside the OwnTech ecosystem.

The objective of OwnTech is to reduce this complexity by providing a complete
open-source technology suite for power electronics. This suite combines
standard and reprogrammable power hardware, a simplified development
environment, software libraries, documentation, and tools for monitoring and
experimentation~\cite{owntech_home,owntech_docs}. Instead of designing a
dedicated converter from zero for each application, the user can start from a
common hardware and software base and adapt it to the required conversion
function.

This open-source approach also concerns the hardware itself. OwnTech highlights
public and editable resources such as design files, bills of materials,
assembly information, CAD files, documentation, and contribution material
~\cite{owntech_home}. This is important for education and research, because
users can inspect the platform, reproduce experiments, and adapt the design to
specific constraints.

The project follows the principle summarized by OwnTech as ``one hardware,
many functions''. A single programmable power electronics platform can be used
for several applications, such as battery charging, photovoltaic energy
conversion, laboratory power supplies, motor control, LED driving, isolated
systems, or small renewable-energy installations~\cite{owntech_home}. This
approach is particularly useful for education, research, and prototyping,
because the same hardware ecosystem can be reused to test different converter
topologies and control strategies.

This accessibility objective can be understood by comparison with other open
electronics platforms. Arduino made embedded electronics accessible to a large
community of users.

The OwnTech approach can also be described as a user-centric technology
sandbox. This sandbox connects different communities, such as academia,
industry, makers, and fablabs, to a common set of tools. These tools include
standard hardware, an intuitive IDE, unified documentation, and open data
monitoring. The goal is not only to provide electronic boards, but to provide
all the elements required to create, test, share, and improve power electronics
applications.

This organization creates impact in two ways. First, it helps new ideas become
real applications in areas such as smart grids, electrical mobility, energy
storage, and renewable energy. Second, it supports learning by doing: students,
engineers, researchers, and makers can experiment on an open platform and
exchange with the community. This is consistent with the OwnTech objective of
giving users the tools they need to act for the energy transition.

\section{OwnTech Boards and Tools}

The OwnTech ecosystem is organized as a modular set of hardware boards and
software tools dedicated to power electronics. Among the main elements
presented by OwnTech are the SPIN control board, the TWIST power module, and
the OwnPlot monitoring interface~\cite{owntech_home,owntech_spin,owntech_twist}.
Other boards and application-specific modules can also be part of this
ecosystem depending on the target converter, the required power level, and the
type of experiment. For example, the OwnVerter board extends the same approach
toward three-phase and motor-control applications. Together, these elements
allow the user to control, power, measure, debug, and monitor converter
applications within a coherent open-source environment.
This corresponds to a software-defined power conversion approach: the hardware
provides the electrical interface, while the software defines the converter
behaviour through PWM generation, measurements, protections, communication, and
control algorithms.

\subsection{Main OwnTech Boards}

OwnTech is built around modular boards and software tools that cover the main
needs of a power converter: control, power conversion, measurement, debugging,
and monitoring. The main boards presented in this report are SPIN, TWIST, and
OwnVerter. SPIN is the OwnTech power controller board used to generate PWM signals,
acquire measurements, and execute real-time control algorithms. TWIST is a
programmable and stackable power module for DC and AC applications up to
110~V~\cite{owntech_twist}. OwnVerter extends the same approach toward
three-phase motor-control applications. The OwnPlot software interface
completes these boards by allowing the user to interact with the application
and monitor internal variables during operation~\cite{owntech_docs}.

Designing a power converter is not an easy task. With OwnTech, the user works
inside a unified environment where the hardware and software are already
compatible and ready to be customized. A simplified graphical interface helps
the user take control of the converter quickly, while the development
environment allows the application to be adapted to the required use case.

Once the application is ready, it can be deployed in real operating conditions,
and usage data can be collected through the included monitoring platform. The
user also has access to extended documentation, from calculation notes to user
manuals. Finally, the community dimension is important: users can share their
results, discuss problems, and receive support while building their own
applications.

\subsection{SPIN Control Board}

OwnTech presents SPIN as a free, ergonomic, and multi-function power controller
~\cite{owntech_spin}. Its main interest is that the same control board can be
reused with different power stages, while keeping a common software environment
and development workflow.

Compared with a conventional microcontroller development board, SPIN provides functions that are
directly useful for switched-mode converters: PWM generation, synchronized
measurements, real-time control loops, communication with monitoring tools, and
debugging support. This makes it suitable for applications such as DC/DC
converters, inverters, motor drives, battery chargers, and laboratory
prototypes.

\subsubsection{Typical SPIN Workflow}

The SPIN board is designed to reduce the number of steps required to start
controlling a power converter. The intended workflow is simple: the user
connects the board through USB-C, downloads the OwnTech code and examples from
GitLab, then programs the board to drive the power stage. This workflow is
important because it lowers the entry barrier for users who want to experiment
with digital power electronics without building a complete control environment
from scratch.

\subsubsection{Role During the Internship}

During this internship, the SPIN board was central to the first PCB project.
Its GPIO and PWM signals had to be validated through the SPOC oscilloscope
shield. The validation bench uses a SPIN board under test to generate
electrical signals, while another control stage selects which signals are
routed toward the oscilloscope. The SPOC board therefore contributes directly
to the validation of SPIN-based hardware and software behaviour.

In the second project, the same control needs appear at a higher switching
performance level: accurate PWM generation, dead-time control, isolation, and
safe switching sequences are required for the GaN half-bridge board.

\subsection{TWIST Power Module}

TWIST is the OwnTech reprogrammable power module. It is designed as a
bidirectional 300~W converter with two low-side channels and one high-side
channel. According to the TWIST datasheet, the low-side voltage range is
12--72~V, the high-side voltage range is up to 100~V in normal operation, and
the board can deliver up to 8~A per low-side channel or 16~A when the channels
are used in parallel.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.62\textwidth]{twist.jpeg}
\caption{OwnTech TWIST power module.}
\label{fig:twist_board}
\end{figure}

The board can be used in several configurations. The two low-side channels may
operate independently to provide two outputs, or they may be combined to
increase the available current. TWIST can be used for DC/DC buck conversion,
boost conversion, independent output operation, low-voltage inverter
experiments, and power-factor-correction studies. This makes it a practical
platform for learning, prototyping, and validating power electronics control
functions.

TWIST is compatible with the SPIN board or other programming systems and
supports communication through CAN-bus and RS-485. Its measurement chain
provides observability of voltages, currents, and temperatures, which is
important for closed-loop control and experimental validation.

\subsection{OwnVerter Board}

OwnVerter extends the OwnTech ecosystem toward higher power and three-phase
applications. It is described in its datasheet as a reprogrammable 1.3~kW
three-phase converter designed mainly for motor-control applications. Unlike
TWIST, which is more general-purpose and modular, OwnVerter is optimized for
three-phase operation and can be used with BLDC motor-control examples and
field-oriented-control libraries.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{ownverter.png}
\caption{OwnTech OwnVerter three-phase power converter.}
\label{fig:ownverter_board}
\end{figure}

The board includes three low-side power channels, one high-side DC bus, and
selectable current sensing ranges. It can interface with Hall-effect sensors,
incremental encoders, and Sin/Cos encoders, which makes it suitable for precise
motor feedback control. It also supports communication through CAN-bus and
RS-485, allowing it to be integrated into a larger experimental setup.

OwnVerter therefore shows how the same OwnTech approach can be extended from
general converter experiments to more advanced motor-drive applications.

\section{Internship Context}

The internship subject is also connected to a larger evolution of OwnTech
hardware. After low-voltage modular converters, the next development step is to
move toward higher voltage and higher power applications. The internship
subject targets converter modules in the range of 400--800~V and 1--10~kW,
with applications such as photovoltaic inverters, motor drives, and other
high-power energy conversion systems.

This evolution introduces new technical constraints. At higher voltage and
higher power, the design must consider insulation, creepage and clearance,
thermal limits, electromagnetic compatibility, safety, and pre-certification
requirements. The use of wide-bandgap devices such as GaN and SiC can improve
switching performance and efficiency, but it also makes PCB layout, gate
driving, parasitic effects, and measurement quality much more critical.

In this context, the internship work was organized around two different PCB
projects. Although these two boards are different, they both support the same
global objective: improving the development and validation workflow of
open-source power electronics hardware. The first project focused on automated
validation, because reliable and repeatable tests are necessary before scaling
hardware platforms. The second one concerned a GaN half-bridge board intended
for high-speed power switching experiments.

The first board is the SPIN Oscilloscope Shield, called SPOC. It is an
oscilloscope interface board used to route selected signals from a SPIN board
under test to oscilloscope inputs. It allows up to four selected GPIO or PWM
signals to be observed at the same time on a standard four-channel
oscilloscope. This board contributes to the automated test bench by making
physical signal validation more systematic and less dependent on manual
probing. The oscilloscope measurements provide feedback to the test software,
which can then decide whether the tested signal passes or fails according to
the expected electrical behaviour.

The second board is a GaN-based half-bridge board. It is intended as an
experimental platform for high-speed switching tests and future high-voltage
converter development. The board integrates digital isolation, gate-driver
stages, an isolated floating supply for the high-side driver, two 650~V GaN
transistors, and a compact DC-link path. Its design is linked to the future
high-power direction of OwnTech, where switching performance, EMI, safety, and
pre-certification become major design concerns.







\section{State of the Art: Sustainable and Modular Power Electronics}
\label{sec:sustainable_modular_power_electronics}

Power electronics is one of the enabling technologies of the energy transition.
It allows electrical energy to be converted, controlled, stored, and exchanged
between photovoltaic panels, batteries, motors, electrical grids, and embedded
systems. However, a power converter is often designed as a dedicated product
for one voltage range, one power level, one topology, and one control strategy.
When the application evolves, this type of closed design can become difficult to
inspect, repair, modify, or reuse. Sustainability in power electronics is
therefore not only a question of efficiency. It also depends on documentation,
maintenance, modularity, upgradeability, and the ability to keep hardware useful
during a longer life cycle.

The state-of-the-art discussion focuses on three
complementary concepts: open-source hardware, obsolescence management, and
software-defined modular converters. These concepts provide a useful background
for discussing how modularity, documentation, and validation can support more
sustainable power electronics hardware.

\subsection{Open-Source Hardware as a Sustainability Tool}
\label{subsec:osh_sustainability_tool}

Open-source hardware gives users access to the design information of a physical
system. In practice, this means that the hardware can be operated, studied,
modified, reproduced, manufactured, and sold under the conditions of the
associated licence. Bonvoisin et al. explain that open-source hardware requires
standardised documentation practices in order to make these rights usable in
practice~\cite{bonvoisin2020standardisation}. A schematic or a PCB file alone is
not enough: users also need interface descriptions, assembly information,
manufacturing data, software, tests, safety limits, and maintenance-oriented
information.

For power electronics, this openness has a direct technical value. A converter
combines power semiconductors, gate drivers, sensing circuits, protection
functions, embedded control, communication, thermal design, and electromagnetic
compatibility constraints. If these elements are documented, the converter is
not only a product to be used; it also becomes a technical object that can be
understood, validated, repaired, and adapted by engineers, researchers,
students, and makers.

\subsection{Obsolescence in Power Electronics}
\label{subsec:obsolescence_power_electronics}

Obsolescence describes the situation in which a product, component, or system
becomes outdated, unavailable, unsuitable, or less useful with respect to current
needs. In industrial systems, obsolescence management is also treated as a
life-cycle activity intended to anticipate and reduce the impact of unavailable
or outdated items. Mellal identifies several forms of
obsolescence, including technical, functional, planned, psychological, and
optional obsolescence~\cite{mellal2020obsolescence}. In power electronics,
technical and functional obsolescence are the most critical for industrial and
research platforms.

    \label{fig:power_electronics_obsolescence}

Figure~\ref{fig:power_electronics_obsolescence} summarizes the main forms of
obsolescence that can affect a power electronics system. Technical obsolescence
comes from component unavailability, reference discontinuation, or supplier
disappearance. Functional obsolescence appears when the converter still works,
but no longer matches the required power level, energy flow, or conversion type.
Planned obsolescence is mainly related to sizing choices made during design, for
example with electrolytic capacitors whose lifetime depends strongly on
temperature and electrical stress.

The figure also includes psychological and optional obsolescence. Psychological
obsolescence is linked to footprint, weight, or style, and is mostly a B2C
criterion, although it can also influence the perceived usability of a platform.
Optional obsolescence occurs when a converter cannot adapt to changes in its
environment, for example because its interfaces, sensing possibilities, control
software, or hardware architecture are too fixed. This is important for OwnTech,
whose objective is to build reusable converter platforms that can be maintained
and adapted instead of replaced as black boxes.

\subsection{Modularity and Software-Defined Power Conversion}
\label{subsec:modularity_sdpc}

Modularity is one way to reduce the impact of obsolescence. Instead of
redesigning or replacing a complete converter, the system can be separated into
functional blocks with clear interfaces: control, sensing, communication,
isolation, gate driving, power switching, filtering, and load interface. If one
block becomes obsolete, limited, or defective, it can be redesigned or replaced
without changing the whole platform.

This idea is related to the Power Electronic Building Block (PEBB) concept,
where power converters are organised as reusable bricks integrating switching
devices, drivers, sensing, protection, thermal design, and local control. Several
bricks can then be combined into a Power Converter Array (PCA) to scale voltage,
current, power, redundancy, or topology. OwnTech also connects this logic to
Software Defined Power Conversion (SDPC): the hardware remains generic or
modular, while the software defines PWM generation, control loops, protections,
operating modes, and sometimes the effective conversion function.

Rahmani discuss the opportunities of modularity for the eco-design of
power converters, especially through repair, reuse, and replacement of
sub-parts~\cite{rahmani2021modularity}. This is particularly relevant in power
electronics because the stressed elements of a converter do not age at the same
speed. Power semiconductors, gate drivers, DC-link capacitors, filters, thermal
interfaces, control electronics, and communication circuits may all have
different life-cycle constraints.

In the context of this internship, this modularity principle is directly linked
to the separation between the control and communication functions, the
instrumentation functions, the isolation stage, the gate-driver stage, and the
GaN half-bridge power stage. This separation makes the system easier to analyse
and validate, and it also makes future upgrades more practical. For example, a
new power stage could be developed to test another GaN or SiC device while
keeping the same control and instrumentation environment. Conversely, the
control or communication board could evolve without requiring a complete redesign
of the power stage.

\subsection{Documentation and Maintenance}
\label{subsec:documentation_maintenance}

A major maintenance issue in power electronics is that converters, especially
inverters, are often treated as black boxes. When a fault appears, the complete
unit is frequently replaced because the internal design, diagnostic information,
and repair procedure are not available. Open-source hardware can help reduce
this problem only if the documentation covers the full life cycle of the
product, not only its manufacturing phase.

This representation is directly related to the modular approach used in the
internship work. A converter can be viewed as an assembly of functional blocks:
control, instrumentation, gate driving, power semiconductors, filtering, and
load interface. If one block becomes defective or obsolete, the objective is to
replace or redesign only this block instead of replacing the complete converter.
This approach supports repairability, upgradeability, and reduction of material
waste, provided that the interfaces, diagnostic points, replacement options, and
maintenance information of each block are documented clearly.

\subsection{Link with the Internship Work}
\label{subsec:internship_positioning_sustainability}

This link with the state of the art is mainly visible through the GaN
half-bridge board. The board is intended as a reusable experimental power stage
for high-speed and high-voltage switching tests. It follows the modular logic
described above by separating the control board, measurement interface,
isolation, gate-driver stage, and power stage.

This separation makes the system easier to understand, validate, modify, and
upgrade. Instead of redesigning one large board, several parameters can be
adjusted by changing only the block or small board concerned. For example, the
power stage can evolve toward another GaN or SiC device while keeping the same
control and measurement environment. Its power capability can also be adapted by
replacing the selected MOSFET with a more powerful device, or by studying
series-connected GaN MOSFETs when higher voltage or power levels are required.
In this sense, the internship contributes to the OwnTech objective of building
power electronics hardware that is more open, modular, maintainable, and
adaptable.
